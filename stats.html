How to Use the Solving Aids

← Back to the solver

In a simple-substitution cryptogram, each letter of the alphabet is consistently replaced by another. The statistics below don't decode the puzzle for you — they narrow the possibilities so you can make smart guesses. Confirm each guess by checking that the words it produces make sense.

Letter frequency

The count and percentage of every cipher letter. English text has a very predictable frequency profile, so the most common cipher letters are likely to be the most common English letters.

  • Typical English order, most to least common: E T A O I N S H R D L U…
  • E alone is usually ~12–13% of all letters — often the single most frequent.
  • The rarest letters are J Q X Z; a cipher letter appearing once or twice is probably one of these.

Digraphs (2-letter sequences)

Pairs of adjacent letters within a word. The cipher digraphs that recur most often likely correspond to common English pairs.

  • Most common English digraphs: TH HE IN ER AN RE ON AT EN.
  • TH is by far the most frequent — pair this with finding THE in the trigraphs.

Trigraphs (3-letter sequences)

Three adjacent letters within a word. A highly recurring trigraph is a strong anchor.

  • Most common English trigraphs: THE AND ING ION ENT HER.
  • A three-letter word that is the most common word in the text is almost always THE.
  • A trigraph ending many words is often ING.

Double letters

Two identical letters in a row. Only some letters commonly double in English.

  • Common doubles: LL SS EE OO TT FF RR NN PP.
  • Doubles almost never use A I U Y H J K Q V W X — you can rule those out.

Reversals

A letter pair that appears in both orders, e.g. the cipher shows both QW and WQ. These often correspond to reversible English pairs.

  • Common reversible pairs: ER/RE, ON/NO, ES/SE, ED/DE, ST/TS.
  • One of the two letters in a reversal is frequently a vowel.

ABA combinations

A three-letter pattern where the first and last letters are the same and the middle differs (e.g. cipher QWQ). The repeated outer letter is usually a vowel.

  • Common ABA patterns: EVE EYE ERE ELE (often outer E) and ASA OSO.
  • Use these to pin down vowel placement once frequency gives you candidates.

One- and two-letter words

Word shapes are preserved, so short words are giveaways:

  • A one-letter word is A or I.
  • Common two-letter words: OF TO IN IS IT BE AS AT SO ON.
  • An apostrophe followed by one letter is usually 'S or 'T; by two, 'RE / 'LL / 'VE.
